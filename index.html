I make ice cream as a hobby. Ice cream often contains eggs. Some people, especially young children, react negatively to learning this. However, not only are many other beloved desserts similarly composed of custards (dairy thickened by adding egg yolks and cooking gently), but the creation of ice cream from such lends it a richer flavor, a smoother texture, and a resistance to freezer burn. The classic egg-containing recipe is often termed "French custard" (hence "French vanilla" ice cream), but egg yolks are just as at home in many upscale gelatos. The term "Philadelphia-style," typically referenced in contrast to "French" custards, is often associated with ice cream made without eggs. However, historically, it indicates a lack of cooking; some older "Philadelphia-style" recipes even contain raw eggs. In the United States, frozen desserts termed "sorbet" are largely assumed to be vegan, even containing no dairy. Before modern concerns over raw eggs, however, many U.S. sorbet recipes called for uncooked egg whites as a stabilizer. I've found it most effective to begin a French custard without the cream, just the smaller portion of milk typically included in the recipe; waiting to add the cream until after the custard has already thickened reduces the risk of "splitting" the cream and retains more of the fresh flavors favored in Philadelphia-style.
